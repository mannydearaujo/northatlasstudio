# Offer Presentation Rules

An offer is what the visitor GETS for acting, stated concretely. "Contact Us" is not an offer. Most local service sites don't have a conversion problem — they have an offer-clarity problem.

## The offer formula (every primary CTA area)
**Action + what they get + friction remover.**
- "Get your free gutter quote — most quotes back within 24 hours."
- "Book Bella's groom online — takes about a minute."
- "Call now — we answer during business hours, no phone tree."

## The three anxieties (answer each within one viewport of the CTA)
1. **Price anxiety:** a band, a starting-at, or the honest factors ("most 5-inch jobs run $8–12/ft depending on fascia condition"). Silence on price reads as "expensive AND evasive."
2. **Timing anxiety:** how fast do they hear back, how far out is scheduling ("quotes within 24 hours; most installs within 2 weeks").
3. **Commitment anxiety:** what saying yes to THIS step means ("free quote, no obligation — if we're not the right fit, we'll say so").

## CTA copy rules
- Verb-first, specific, first-person-benefit framing where natural ("Get my quote").
- One primary action per page; secondary actions styled visibly weaker (text links, outline buttons).
- Never "Submit", "Learn More" (as primary), or "Contact Us" when "Get a Quote" is the truth.
- Match the CTA to intent temperature: location/service pages (hot) get quote/call CTAs; blog posts (warm) get the related service page, not a hard sell.

## Legitimate urgency (the honest kind)
Seasonal reality ("leaf season books out — October slots go fast"), real capacity ("we take 4 installs a week"), real deadlines. If the owner can't verify it, it doesn't run. Fake countdowns and manufactured scarcity are banned (see guardrails) — local reputations die on stunts.
