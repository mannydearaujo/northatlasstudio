# Effort vs. impact framework

Every recommended fix lands in exactly one of two buckets. The point of the framework is to keep the
free AI Operations Check honest about what can close on the spot versus what needs a real follow-up.

## Quick wins — high impact, low effort

Defining trait: **the owner (or their existing staff) could realistically be using the fix within
days, with no bespoke build.** This is almost always:

- one of the three cataloged AI Operations installs, once scoped, or
- a plain SaaS/AI tool sign-up that directly replaces a manual step.

These are the fixes the free 20-minute call can reasonably close live, because the deliverable is
concrete and the cost/effort is small and known up front.

## Bigger opportunities — high impact, higher effort

Defining trait: **the fix requires more than "sign up and start using it"** — it needs a scoped
build, a process change, or ongoing configuration. This includes:

- process redesign (fixing a broken workflow before automating it),
- a custom knowledge system or trained assistant built around the business's own material,
- any bespoke automation that isn't a straightforward off-the-shelf tool.

These are not closed live on the free call — they're the reason a follow-up conversation (and, when
ready, an `offer-sheet-builder` pitch) exists. Flag them clearly as "worth a longer conversation," not
as something the owner should expect to have running by next week.

## What does NOT belong on the matrix

- **Low impact, any effort** — if a bottleneck the owner described doesn't actually cost them
  meaningful time or money, don't manufacture urgency around fixing it. Note it briefly if relevant,
  but don't build a recommendation around it just to fill out the report.
- **Anything that can't be tied to something the owner actually said.** A recommendation without a
  real bottleneck behind it isn't a finding — it's a guess.
